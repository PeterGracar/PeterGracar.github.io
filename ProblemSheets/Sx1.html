Problem 1.1.

What is the price of an option with payoff h⁢(S1)=S1h(S_{1})=S_{1}?

Solution The price of the option will be equal to S0S_{0}. One explanation is using replicating strategies. The obvious replicating strategy is (x=S0,ϕ=1)(x=S_{0},\phi=1). Therefore the option should also have price S0S_{0}. Alternatively, one can use risk-neutral probabilities, namely p~=1+r−du−d\tilde{p}=\frac{1+r-d}{u-d} and 1−p~=u−1−ru−d1-\tilde{p}=\frac{u-1-r}{u-d}. To calculate the price we have:
Eℙ~⁢(h⁢(S1)1+r)=Eℙ~⁢(S11+r)=S0,E_{\tilde{\mathbb{P}}}\Big{(}\frac{h(S_{1})}{1+r}\Big{)}=E_{\tilde{\mathbb{P}}% }\Big{(}\frac{S_{1}}{1+r}\Big{)}=S_{0},
by the properties of the risk-neutral measure.

Problem 1.4.

Consider the elementary single period market model with parameters: r=13r=\frac{1}{3}, S0=1S_{0}=1, u=2u=2, d=12d=\frac{1}{2}, p=34p=\frac{3}{4}. Compute the price of a digital call option with strike price KK which is characterized by the payoff function

h⁢(S1)={1,if ⁢S1≥K,0,otherwise.h(S_{1})=\left\{\begin{array}[]{ll}1,&\text{if }S_{1}\geq K,\\ 0,&\text{otherwise.}\end{array}\right.

Notice that the price depends on a real number KK which is a parameter of the option.

Solution S1S_{1} is as in the previous exercise. If K>2K>2 we have that the payoff of the digital option is always 0, so the replicating strategy is (x,ϕ)=(0,0)(x,\phi)=(0,0). If 12<K≤2\frac{1}{2}<K\leq 2, the payoff is 11 in HH and 0 in TT. This gives the system of equations
(x−ϕ)⁢43+ϕ⁢2=1\displaystyle(x-\phi)\frac{4}{3}+\phi 2=1
(x−ϕ)⁢43+12⁢ϕ=0\displaystyle(x-\phi)\frac{4}{3}+\frac{1}{2}\phi=0

ϕ=12−12=23\phi=\frac{1}{2-\frac{1}{2}}=\frac{2}{3}
and so x=512x=\frac{5}{12}. If K≤12K\leq\frac{1}{2}, the payoff is always 11. Calculating as above, ϕ=0\phi=0 and x=34x=\frac{3}{4}.

Problem 1.5.

Give a proof of the Put-Call Parity.

Solution The formula:
C−P=S0−11+r⁢K.C-P=S_{0}-\frac{1}{1+r}K.
Consider two portfolios.
  • •
    ​

    Portfolio 1: 1 call option + K⁢11+rK\frac{1}{1+r} in cash

    At time t=1t=1 this portfolio has has value

    (S1−K)++K={S1−K+K,S1>K,0+K,S1≤K.(S_{1}-K)^{+}+K=\begin{cases}S_{1}-K+K,&S_{1}>K,\\ 0+K,&S_{1}\leq K.\end{cases}
  • •
    ​

    Portfolio 2: 1 put option + 1 share of stock

    At time t=1t=1 this portfolio has has value

    (K−S1)++S1={S1,S1>KK−S1+S1,S1≤K(K-S_{1})^{+}+S_{1}=\begin{cases}S_{1},&S_{1}>K\\ K-S_{1}+S_{1},&S_{1}\leq K\end{cases}

Problem 1.6.

Under the assumption that the elementary single period market model is arbitrage free show that there exists a random variable ZZ such that the price of an European option with the payoff h⁢(S1)h(S_{1}) can be computed by the formula

x=𝔼⁢(Z⁢h⁢(S1)).x=\mathbb{E}\left(Zh(S_{1})\right).
Solution Assuming no arbitrage gives us that there exists a risk-neutral measure. We want to show that there exists a ZZ s.t.
x=𝔼⁢(Z⁢h⁢(S1))x=\mathbb{E}(Zh(S_{1}))
and
x=𝔼ℙ~⁢(h⁢(S1)1+r)x=\mathbb{E}_{\tilde{\mathbb{P}}}\big{(}\frac{h(S_{1})}{1+r}\big{)}
The second equation can be expressed as
x=11+r(p~h(S1(H))+(1−p~)h(S1(T))x=\frac{1}{1+r}(\tilde{p}h(S_{1}(H))+(1-\tilde{p})h(S_{1}(T))
while the first equation gives
x=p⁢Z⁢(H)⁢h⁢(S1⁢(H))+(1−p)⁢Z⁢(T)⁢h⁢(S1⁢(T))x=pZ(H)h(S_{1}(H))+(1-p)Z(T)h(S_{1}(T))
Combining the two gives that if we set
Z⁢(H)=p~p⁢11+rZ(H)=\frac{\tilde{p}}{p}\frac{1}{1+r}
and
Z⁢(T)=1−p~1−p⁢11+r,Z(T)=\frac{1-\tilde{p}}{1-p}\frac{1}{1+r},
ZZ satisfies the requirement. Such a ZZ is called the Radon-Nikodym derivative or the State-Price density.
Problem 1.7.

Consider the elementary single period market model with parameters r=13r=\frac{1}{3}, S0=1S_{0}=1, u=2u=2, d=12d=\frac{1}{2}, p=34p=\frac{3}{4}. Find all probability measures ℚ\mathbb{Q} that assign positive values to both states HH and TT and satisfy

S0=𝔼ℚ⁢S11+r,S_{0}=\mathbb{E}_{\mathbb{Q}}\frac{S_{1}}{1+r},

where

𝔼ℚ⁢S11+r=S1⁢(H)1+r⁢ℚ⁢(H)+S1⁢(T)1+r⁢ℚ⁢(T).\mathbb{E}_{\mathbb{Q}}\frac{S_{1}}{1+r}=\frac{S_{1}(H)}{1+r}\mathbb{Q}(H)+% \frac{S_{1}(T)}{1+r}\mathbb{Q}(T).
Solution ℚ\mathbb{Q} is a probability measure so ℚ⁢(H)+ℚ⁢(t)=1\mathbb{Q}(H)+\mathbb{Q}(t)=1. We also want ℚ⁢(H)\mathbb{Q}(H) and ℚ⁢(T)\mathbb{Q}(T) to be strictly positive. Since ℚ\mathbb{Q} satisfies S0=𝔼ℚ⁢S11+rS_{0}=\mathbb{E}_{\mathbb{Q}}\frac{S_{1}}{1+r} by assumption, this gives that
1=11+r⁢(ℚ⁢(H)⁢u+ℚ⁢(T)⁢d).1=\frac{1}{1+r}(\mathbb{Q}(H)u+\mathbb{Q}(T)d).
Combined with ℚ⁢(H)+ℚ⁢(t)=1\mathbb{Q}(H)+\mathbb{Q}(t)=1 this gives
ℚ⁢(T)=u−1−ru−d\mathbb{Q}(T)=\frac{u-1-r}{u-d}
and
ℚ⁢(H)=1+r−du−d.\mathbb{Q}(H)=\frac{1+r-d}{u-d}.
In our example this gives ℚ⁢(H)=59>0\mathbb{Q}(H)=\frac{5}{9}>0 and ℚ⁢(T)=49>0\mathbb{Q}(T)=\frac{4}{9}>0, so this is the unique solution to the problem.
