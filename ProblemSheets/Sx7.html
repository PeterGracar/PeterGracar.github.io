Problem 7.1.

Show that the following Utility Functions satisfy the properties 1−31-3 from Definition 3.1.1 in the Notes. Calculate the coefficients of risk aversion (absolute and relative).

  1. (a)
    ​

    u⁢(x)=5⁢ln⁡(x)u(x)=5\ln(x).

  2. (b)
    ​

    u⁢(x)=x1/3u(x)={x}^{1/3} (it is an example of the power utility function).

Solution
  1. (a)
    ​

    We consider u⁢(x)=5⁢ln⁡(x)u(x)=5\ln(x) with x>0x>0. The first derivative is u′⁢(x)=5x>0u^{\prime}(x)=\frac{5}{x}>0, still for x>0x>0, so condition 2 is satisfied. The second derivative is u′′⁢(x)=−5x2<0u^{\prime\prime}(x)=-\frac{5}{x^{2}}<0 for x>0x>0 and so the 3rd condition is satisfied.

    Furthermore,

    limx↓0u′⁢(x)=limx↓05x=∞\lim_{x\downarrow 0}u^{\prime}(x)=\lim_{x\downarrow 0}\frac{5}{x}=\infty

    and

    limx↑∞u′⁢(x)=limx↑∞5x=0,\lim_{x\uparrow\infty}u^{\prime}(x)=\lim_{x\uparrow\infty}\frac{5}{x}=0,

    so condition 1 holds. We next calculate the two coefficients of risk aversion:

    α⁢(x)=−u′′⁢(x)u′⁢(x)=−−5x25x=1x,\alpha(x)=-\frac{u^{\prime\prime}(x)}{u^{\prime}(x)}=-\frac{-\frac{5}{x^{2}}}{% \frac{5}{x}}=\frac{1}{x},

    which shows decreasing absolute risk aversion (DARA).

    r⁢(x)=−x⁢u′′⁢(x)u′⁢(x)=1,r(x)=-\frac{xu^{\prime\prime}(x)}{u^{\prime}(x)}=1,

    which shows a constant relative risk aversion (CRRA).

  2. (b)
    ​

    We repeat the above for u⁢(x)=x1/3u(x)=x^{1/3}. This gives us:

    u′⁢(x)=13⁢x−2/3>0,x>0u^{\prime}(x)=\frac{1}{3}x^{-2/3}>0,\quad x>0

    and therefore uu is increasing.

    u′′⁢(x)=−29⁢x−5/3<0,x>0u^{\prime\prime}(x)=-\frac{2}{9}x^{-5/3}<0,\quad x>0

    and thus uu is concave.

    limx↓0u′⁢(x)=limx↓013⁢x2/3=∞\lim_{x\downarrow 0}u^{\prime}(x)=\lim_{x\downarrow 0}\frac{1}{3x^{2/3}}=\infty

    and

    limx↑∞u′⁢(x)=limx↑∞13⁢x2/3=0\lim_{x\uparrow\infty}u^{\prime}(x)=\lim_{x\uparrow\infty}\frac{1}{3x^{2/3}}=0

    and consequently condition 1 holds. Finally,

    α⁢(x)=−−29⁢x−5/313⁢x−2/3=23⁢x−1\alpha(x)=-\frac{-\frac{2}{9}x^{-5/3}}{\frac{1}{3}x^{-2/3}}=\frac{2}{3}x^{-1}

    and

    r⁢(x)=23,r(x)=\frac{2}{3},

    so we again have DARA and CRRA.

  2. (b)
    ​

    When is a contingent claim called attainable? In the general single-period market model, are all contingent claims attainable when the market is arbitrage-free?

  3. (c)
    ​

    In a single period model with three states of the world (Ω={ω1,ω2,ω3}\Omega=\{\omega_{1},\omega_{2},\omega_{3}\}), one stock and a money market account available, we are given the set 𝕄\mathbb{M} of risk neutral measures:

    𝕄={(λ1−32⁢λ12⁢λ),λ∈(0,23)}.\mathbb{M}=\{\left(\begin{array}[]{c}\lambda\\ 1-\frac{3}{2}\lambda\\ \frac{1}{2}\lambda\end{array}\right),\leavevmode\nobreak\ \lambda\in(0,\frac{2% }{3})\}.

    Give an example of an attainable contingent claim in this market.

  4. (d)
    ​

Solution
  1. (a)
    ​

    We first check that ℙ~⁢(H)+ℙ~⁢(T)=1\tilde{\mathbb{P}}(H)+\tilde{\mathbb{P}}(T)=1, which can quickly be verified to hold. We also have that ℙ~⁢(H)>0\tilde{\mathbb{P}}(H)>0 and ℙ~⁢(T)>0\tilde{\mathbb{P}}(T)>0 since d<1+r<ud<1+r<u. It remains to show that this probability measure is risk neutral. We calculate

    𝔼ℙ~⁢(S11+r)\displaystyle\mathbb{E}_{\tilde{\mathbb{P}}}(\frac{S_{1}}{1+r}) =11+r⁢(ℙ~⁢(H)⁢S1⁢(H)+ℙ~⁢(T)⁢S1⁢(T))\displaystyle=\frac{1}{1+r}(\tilde{\mathbb{P}}(H)S_{1}(H)+\tilde{\mathbb{P}}(T% )S_{1}(T))
    =11+r⁢(1+r−du−d⁢S0⁢u+u−1−ru−d⁢S0⁢d)\displaystyle=\frac{1}{1+r}(\frac{1+r-d}{u-d}S_{0}u+\frac{u-1-r}{u-d}S_{0}d)
    =S01+r⁢(u−r⁢u−d⁢u+d⁢u−d−r⁢du−d)\displaystyle=\frac{S_{0}}{1+r}(\frac{u-ru-du+du-d-rd}{u-d})
    =S01+r⁢(u−d)⁢(1+r)u−d\displaystyle=\frac{S_{0}}{1+r}\frac{(u-d)(1+r)}{u-d}
    =S0.\displaystyle=S_{0}.

    Note: This makes the discounted stock price a martingale (a stochastic process that in expectation maintains its value).

  2. (b)
    ​

    A contingent claim is attainable if and only if there exists a replicating strategy. A market being arbitrage-free does not necessarily mean complete. Therefore, in a market that is arbitrage-free but not complete, not every contingent claim is attainable.

  3. (c)
    ​

    This market is incomplete because we have more than 1 risk-neutral measure. We want to find a contingent claim that is attainable. This means that a replicating strategy exists for this claim, which is equivalent to the price of the claim being the same for all risk-neutral measures, i.e. the price must not depend on λ\lambda. If we calculate the price of a general claim, it holds that

    𝔼ℚ⁢(X1+r)\displaystyle\mathbb{E}_{\mathbb{Q}}(\frac{X}{1+r}) =11+r⁢(λ⁢X⁢(ω1)+(1−32⁢λ)⁢X⁢(ω2)+12⁢λ⁢X⁢(ω3))\displaystyle=\frac{1}{1+r}(\lambda X(\omega_{1})+(1-\frac{3}{2}\lambda)X(% \omega_{2})+\frac{1}{2}\lambda X(\omega_{3}))
    =λ⁢11+r⁢(X⁢(ω1)−32⁢X⁢(ω2)+12⁢X⁢(ω3))+X⁢(ω2)1+r.\displaystyle=\lambda\frac{1}{1+r}(X(\omega_{1})-\frac{3}{2}X(\omega_{2})+% \frac{1}{2}X(\omega_{3}))+\frac{X(\omega_{2})}{1+r}.

    Therefore, if X⁢(ω1)−32⁢X⁢(ω2)+12⁢X⁢(ω3)=0X(\omega_{1})-\frac{3}{2}X(\omega_{2})+\frac{1}{2}X(\omega_{3})=0, the price does not depend on λ\lambda and the claim is therefore attainable.
