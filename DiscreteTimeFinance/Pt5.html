In order to specify our market model, we still have to define what trading strategies the agents in our model are allowed to use in order to trade on the market. As in Chapter 1 we assume that the market consists of a money market account (e.g. a bond) denoted by BB, which evolves deterministically as Bt+k=Bt⁢(1+r)kB_{t+k}=B_{t}(1+r)^{k}, where rr is the interest rate (we can assume that B0=1B_{0}=1 without loss of generality), and nn stocks S1,…,SnS^{1},\ldots,S^{n}, which are assumed to be stochastic processes on the underlying state space. A trading strategy is then given by an ℝn+1\mathbb{R}^{n+1} valued stochastic process (ϕt)t=0,…,T(\phi_{t})_{t=0,\ldots,T} whose components are denoted ϕt0,ϕt1,…,ϕtn\phi_{t}^{0},\phi_{t}^{1},...,\phi_{t}^{n} and where ϕti\phi_{t}^{i} denotes the number of shares of the ii-th stock hold at time tt and ϕt0\phi_{t}^{0} the number of bonds at time tt.

Definition 2.1.13.

The value process corresponding to the trading strategy ϕ=(ϕt)0≤t≤T−1\phi=(\phi_{t})_{0\leq t\leq T-1} is the stochastic process (Vt⁢(ϕ))0≤t≤T\big{(}V_{t}(\phi)\big{)}_{0\leq t\leq T}, where

Vt⁢(ϕ)=ϕt0⁢Bt+∑i=1nϕti⁢Sti,t=0,…,T.

It is unreasonable to allow all trading strategies. In particular, the choice of investment should be based on the available information and the capital in possession.

Definition 2.1.14.

A trading strategy ϕ=(ϕt)0≤t≤T\phi=(\phi_{t})_{0\leq t\leq T} is called (ℱt)(\mathcal{F}_{t})-adapted, if the stochastic process (ϕt)0≤t≤T(\phi_{t})_{0\leq t\leq T} is adapted to the filtration (ℱt)0≤t≤T(\mathcal{F}_{t})_{0\leq t\leq T}.

Definition 2.1.15.

A trading strategy ϕ=(ϕt)0≤t≤T\phi=(\phi_{t})_{0\leq t\leq T} is called self financing, if for all t=0,…,T−1t=0,...,T-1

ϕt0Bt+1+∑i=1nϕtiSt+1i=ϕt+10Bt+1+∑i=1nϕt+1iSt+1i.\boxed{\displaystyle\phi_{t}^{0}B_{t+1}+\sum_{i=1}^{n}\phi_{t}^{i}S_{t+1}^{i}=% \phi_{t+1}^{0}B_{t+1}+\sum_{i=1}^{n}\phi_{t+1}^{i}S_{t+1}^{i}.} (2.1.3)

An agent who invests according to a self-financing portfolio, invests money into the strategy in time t=0t=0 and in the following moments only rebalances his portfolio, neither withdrawing any money out of the market, nor investing new money into the market. The agent chooses his investments at time tt and does not change his portfolio in the time interval [t,t+1)[t,t+1). The left-hand side of equation (2.1.3) represents the value of the agents portfolio immediately before time t+1t+1, whereas the right-hand side represents the value of his portfolio immediately after time t+1t+1, when rebalancing of his portfolio has taken place. The self financing assumption says that these two values must be equal, and this means that neither money is withdrawn nor new money is invested.

We are now able to set up our general multi period market model:

Definition 2.1.16.

A general multi period market model is given by the following data:

  1. (1)
    ​

    A probability space (Ω,ℙ)(\Omega,\mathbb{P}) with the filtration (ℱt)t=0,…,T(\mathcal{F}_{t})_{t=0,\ldots,T}.

  2. (2)
    ​

    A money market account (Bt)(B_{t}), which evolves according to Bt=(1+r)tB_{t}=(1+r)^{t}.

  3. (3)
    ​

    A collection of nn financial assets whose prices are given by stochastic processes

    ((St1),…,(Stn))t=0,…,T,\big{(}(S_{t}^{1}),...,(S_{t}^{n})\big{)}_{t=0,\ldots,T},

    which are assumed to be (ℱt)(\mathcal{F}_{t})-adapted.

  4. (4)
    ​

    A set 𝒯\mathcal{T} of self financing and (ℱt)(\mathcal{F}_{t})-adapted trading strategies.

In practical applications the filtration ℱt\mathcal{F}_{t} is often taken to be generated by the prices of assets, so that it represents only the information that can be learnt from the market. Following Definition 2.1.10 we know how to construct such a filtration for one stochastic process (n=1n=1). If n>1n>1, it is easiest to consider the approach following Definition 2.1.10 and to make use of partitions. If the filtration (ℱt)(\mathcal{F}_{t}) is generated by ((St1),…,(Stn))t=0,…,T\big{(}(S_{t}^{1}),...,(S_{t}^{n})\big{)}_{t=0,\ldots,T}, then

ℱt={the information generated by Sji, i=1,…,n, j=0,…,t}.\mathcal{F}_{t}=\{\text{the information generated by $S^{i}_{j}$, $i=1,\ldots,% n$, $j=0,\ldots,t$}\}.

As before we put all sets from the partitions of σ\sigma-algebras generated by SjiS^{i}_{j}, i=1,…,ni=1,\ldots,n, j=0,…,tj=0,\ldots,t into one bag denoted by 𝒢\mathcal{G}. We find the smallest collection of sets (Ai)i∈I(A_{i})_{i\in I}, for some set of indexes II, that satisfies

  1. (1)
    ​

    Ai≠∅︀A_{i}\neq\emptyset for i∈Ii\in I.

  2. (2)
    ​

    Every set G∈𝒢G\in\mathcal{G} can be written as a union of some of the AiA_{i}’s, i.e. G=⋃j∈JAjG=\bigcup_{j\in J}A_{j} with J⊂IJ\subset I.

  3. (3)
    ​

    Sets AiA_{i} are pairwise disjoint, i.e. Ai∩Aj=∅︀A_{i}\cap A_{j}=\emptyset whenever i≠ji\neq j.

We claim that this collection is a partition for ℱt\mathcal{F}_{t} (check it!).

It is often useful to represent the self financing condition in terms of the (multi period) gains process.

Definition 2.1.18.

Assume we are given a general multi period market model as described in Definition 2.1.13. The increment process (Δ⁢Sti)1≤t≤T(\Delta S_{t}^{i})_{1\leq t\leq T} corresponding to the ii-th stock is defined by

ΔSti:=Sti−St−1i,t=1,…,T.\boxed{\displaystyle\Delta S_{t}^{i}:=S^{i}_{t}-S^{i}_{t-1},\qquad t=1,...,T.} (2.1.4)

The increments of the money market account are given by

Δ⁢Bt=Bt−Bt−1=(1+r)t−1⁢r=Bt−1⁢r,t=1,…,T.\Delta B_{t}=B_{t}-B_{t-1}=(1+r)^{t-1}r=B_{t-1}r,\qquad t=1,\ldots,T.

Given a trading strategy ϕ\phi, the corresponding gains process (Gt⁢(ϕ))0≤t≤T(G_{t}(\phi))_{0\leq t\leq T} is given by G0⁢(ϕ)=0G_{0}(\phi)=0 and

Gt(ϕ)=∑s=0t−1ϕsiΔBs+1+∑i=1n∑s=0t−1ϕsiΔSs+1i,t=1,…,T.\boxed{\displaystyle G_{t}(\phi)=\sum_{s=0}^{t-1}\phi_{s}^{i}\Delta B_{s+1}+% \sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{s=0}^{t-1}\phi_{s}^{i}\Delta S^{i}_{s+1},\qquad t=1,\ldots% ,T.} (2.1.5)

As in the single period market models, it is worthwhile to consider discounted counterparts of the price process, gains process and value process:

Definition 2.1.19.

Given a general multi period market model, the discounted prices are given by

S^ti=StiBt,\boxed{\displaystyle\hat{S}_{t}^{i}=\frac{S_{t}^{i}}{B_{t}},} (2.1.6)

the discounted gains by

ΔS^ti=S^ti−S^t−1i,\boxed{\displaystyle\Delta\hat{S}_{t}^{i}=\hat{S}_{t}^{i}-\hat{S}_{t-1}^{i},} (2.1.7)

the discounted value process corresponding to a trading strategy ϕ=(ϕt)0≤t≤T\phi=(\phi_{t})_{0\leq t\leq T} by

V^t(ϕ)=Vt⁢(ϕ)Bt,\boxed{\displaystyle\hat{V}_{t}(\phi)=\frac{V_{t}(\phi)}{B_{t}},} (2.1.8)

and the discounted gains process corresponding to a trading strategy ϕ=(ϕt)0≤t≤T\phi=(\phi_{t})_{0\leq t\leq T} by G^0⁢(ϕ)=0\hat{G}_{0}(\phi)=0 and

G^t(ϕ)=∑i=1n∑s=0t−1ϕsiΔS^s+1i,t=1,…,T.\boxed{\displaystyle\hat{G}_{t}(\phi)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{s=0}^{t-1}\phi_{s}^{% i}\Delta\hat{S}_{s+1}^{i},\qquad t=1,\ldots,T.} (2.1.9)

The self financing condition (2.1.3) states that changes of the amount of capital invested are caused only by gains or losses incurred by investments in the market. Below is a formal statement of this intuition:

Proposition 2.1.20.

An adapted trading strategy ϕ=(ϕt)0≤t≤T\phi=(\phi_{t})_{0\leq t\leq T} is self financing, if and only if any of the two equivalent statements holds

  1. (1)
    ​

    Vt⁢(ϕ)=V0⁢(ϕ)+Gt⁢(ϕ),V_{t}(\phi)=V_{0}(\phi)+G_{t}(\phi), for all 0≤t≤T0\leq t\leq T.

  2. (2)
    ​

    V^t⁢(ϕ)=V^0⁢(ϕ)+G^t⁢(ϕ),\hat{V}_{t}(\phi)=\hat{V}_{0}(\phi)+\hat{G}_{t}(\phi), for all 0≤t≤T0\leq t\leq T.

Proof.

2.2. Properties of the general multi period market model

In this section we redefine the general concepts of financial mathematics, such as arbitrage, hedging etc. in the framework of multi period market models. This is also a good repetition.

Definition 2.2.1.

A trading strategy ϕ=(ϕt)0≤t≤T\phi=(\phi_{t})_{0\leq t\leq T} in a general multi period market model is called an arbitrage if

  1. (1)
    ​

    V0⁢(ϕ)=0V_{0}(\phi)=0

  2. (2)
    ​

    VT⁢(ϕ)≥0V_{T}(\phi)\geq 0

  3. (3)
    ​

    𝔼⁢(VT⁢(ϕ))>0\mathbb{E}(V_{T}(\phi))>0

As in a single period market model condition 3. can be replaced by:

  • (3’)
    ​

    there exists ω∈Ω\omega\in\Omega such that VT⁢(ϕ)⁢(ω)>0V_{T}(\phi)(\omega)>0.

Furthermore one can use the discounted value process or the discounted gains process in order to express the arbitrage conditions.

We come back to the notion of a contingent claim and a hedging (replicating) strategy:

Definition 2.2.2.

A contingent claim in a multi period market model is an ℱT\mathcal{F}_{T}-measurable random variable XX on Ω\Omega representing a payoff at the terminal time TT. A hedging strategy for XX is a trading strategy ϕ∈𝒯\phi\in\mathcal{T} s.t.

VT⁢(ϕ)=X,V_{T}(\phi)=X,

i.e. the terminal value of the trading strategy is equal to the payoff of the contingent claim.

In a multi period market model it is reasonable to look for the price of a contingent claim in any time t<Tt<T. This certainly includes t=0t=0. Therefore, instead of of talking about a price of the contingent claim we shall talk about a price process of the contingent claim. We start with an application of the replication principle. If XX is a contingent claim and ϕ\phi is the hedge for XX then Vt⁢(ϕ)V_{t}(\phi) is a proper price process of XX. We will see later (Proposition 2.4.5) that this is the only price that does not introduce arbitrage opportunities in the market.

Example 2.2.3.

Let us consider the two period model from Example 2.1.11:

We assume that the interest rate is equal to zero, i.e. Bt=1B_{t}=1 for t=0,1,2t=0,1,2. We consider a digital call in this model, which pays 11, if the stock price at time t=2t=2 is greater or equal than 88, i.e.

X⁢(ω)={1,S2⁢(ω)≥8,0,S2⁢(ω)<8.X(\omega)=\begin{cases}1,&S_{2}(\omega)\geq 8,\\ 0,&S_{2}(\omega)<8.\end{cases}

X⁢(ω)={1,ω=ω1,0,otherwise.X(\omega)=\begin{cases}1,&\omega=\omega_{1},\\ 0,&\text{otherwise.}\end{cases}

V2⁢(ϕ)⁢(ω)=ϕ20⁢(ω)+ϕ21⁢(ω)⁢S2⁢(ω)=X⁢(ω).V_{2}(\phi)(\omega)=\phi_{2}^{0}(\omega)+\phi_{2}^{1}(\omega)S_{2}(\omega)=X(% \omega).

ϕ20⁢(ω)+ϕ21⁢(ω)⁢S2⁢(ω)=ϕ10⁢(ω)+ϕ11⁢(ω)⁢S2⁢(ω)\phi_{2}^{0}(\omega)+\phi_{2}^{1}(\omega)S_{2}(\omega)=\phi_{1}^{0}(\omega)+% \phi_{1}^{1}(\omega)S_{2}(\omega)

Therefore if we use self financing trading strategies the terminal share holdings ϕT\phi_{T} does not play any role: e.g. we can simply choose the ϕ20⁢(ω)\phi_{2}^{0}(\omega)’s and ϕ21⁢(ω)\phi_{2}^{1}(\omega)’s equal to the ϕ10⁢(ω)\phi_{1}^{0}(\omega)’s and ϕ11⁢(ω)\phi_{1}^{1}(\omega)’s.

The trick now is to decompose the model into single period market models. In this case we have three, one starting at time t=0t=0 going until time t=1t=1 with price S0=5S_{0}=5, one starting at time t=1t=1 with price S1=8S_{1}=8 and one starting at time t=1t=1 with price S1=4S_{1}=4. Due to the replication principle (and above example) we know a method to price contingent claims for which there exists a hedging strategy. As in general single period market models there may be contingent claims for which there is no hedging strategy in 𝒯\mathcal{T}. We have then analogous definitions as in the single period case.

Definition 2.2.4.

A contingent claim XX is called attainable in 𝒯\mathcal{T}, if there exists a trading strategy ϕ∈𝒯\phi\in\mathcal{T} which replicates XX, i.e. satisfies VT⁢(ϕ)=XV_{T}(\phi)=X.

Definition 2.2.5.

A general multi period market model is called complete, if and only if every contingent claim is attainable. A model that is not complete is called incomplete.

Example 2.2.6.

A multi period version of the stochastic volatility model in Example 1.2.15 is an incomplete multi period market model. ∎

2.3. Conditional expectation

In order to extend the notion of risk neutral probability measure to the multi period case, we need the concept of conditional expectation. This is due to the dynamic character of the multi period model.

Definition 2.3.1.

Let (Ω,ℙ)(\Omega,\mathbb{P}) be a finite probability space, XX a random variable and 𝒢\mathcal{G} a σ\sigma-algebra of sets of Ω\Omega. Denoting the unique partition of 𝒢\mathcal{G} with (Ai)i∈I(A_{i})_{i\in I} the conditional expectation 𝔼⁢(X|𝒢)\mathbb{E}(X|\mathcal{G}) of XX with respect to 𝒢\mathcal{G} is defined as the random variable which satisfies

𝔼(X|𝒢)(ω)=∑xxℙ(X=x|Ai),ω∈Ai.\boxed{\mathbb{E}(X|\mathcal{G})(\omega)=\sum_{x}x\,\mathbb{P}(X=x|A_{i}),% \quad\omega\in A_{i}.} (2.3.1)

Here

ℙ⁢(X=x|Ai)=ℙ⁢({X=x}∩Ai)ℙ⁢(Ai)\mathbb{P}(X=x|A_{i})=\frac{\mathbb{P}(\{X=x\}\cap A_{i})}{\mathbb{P}(A_{i})}

denotes the conditional probability of the event {ω|X⁢(ω)=x}\{\omega|X(\omega)=x\} given the event AiA_{i}.

It is worth to mention that 𝔼⁢(X|𝒢)\mathbb{E}(X|\mathcal{G}) is a random variable. It has the same value on each set of the partition, i.e. 𝔼⁢(X|𝒢)⁢(ω)=𝔼⁢(X|𝒢)⁢(ω′)\mathbb{E}(X|\mathcal{G})(\omega)=\mathbb{E}(X|\mathcal{G})(\omega^{\prime}) if there exists i∈Ii\in I such that ω,ω′∈Ai\omega,\omega^{\prime}\in A_{i}. Notice, however, that for ω\omega’s in different sets of the partition values can be different. Until now it seems that the conditional expectation is a purely artificial construct. Not at all. The intuitive meaning is the following: the conditional expectation gives the average of values of XX (weighted by their probabilities) on each set AiA_{i} of the partition. It is somehow ”the expected value” of XX on each set of the partition of 𝒢\mathcal{G}. It follows from Proposition 2.1.5 that 𝔼⁢(X|𝒢)\mathbb{E}(X|\mathcal{G}) is a 𝒢\mathcal{G}-measurable random variable. The verification of the following identity is left as an exercise: for any G∈𝒢G\in\mathcal{G} one has

∑ω∈GX(ω)ℙ(ω)=∑ω∈G𝔼(X|𝒢)(ω)ℙ(ω).\boxed{\sum_{\omega\in G}X(\omega)\mathbb{P}(\omega)=\sum_{\omega\in G}\mathbb% {E}(X|\mathcal{G})(\omega)\mathbb{P}(\omega).} (2.3.2)
Example 2.3.2.

The following properties of the conditional expectation are very important and useful. The proofs are omitted.

Proposition 2.3.3.

Assume we have a probability space (Ω,ℙ)(\Omega,\mathbb{P}) and σ\sigma-algebras 𝒢\mathcal{G}, 𝒢1\mathcal{G}_{1} and 𝒢2\mathcal{G}_{2} of subsets of Ω\Omega. Assume furthermore that 𝒢2⊂𝒢1\mathcal{G}_{2}\subset\mathcal{G}_{1}. Then

  1. (1)
    ​

    If X:Ω→ℝX:\Omega\to\mathbb{R} is a random variable then

    𝔼⁢(X|𝒢2)=𝔼⁢(𝔼⁢(X|𝒢1)|𝒢2)\boxed{\mathbb{E}(X|\mathcal{G}_{2})=\mathbb{E}(\mathbb{E}(X|\mathcal{G}_{1})|% \mathcal{G}_{2})} (2.3.3)
  2. (2)
    ​

    If Y:Ω→ℝY:\Omega\to\mathbb{R} is a 𝒢\mathcal{G}-measurable random variable, then

    𝔼(YX|𝒢)=Y𝔼(X|𝒢).\boxed{\mathbb{E}(YX|\mathcal{G})=Y\,\mathbb{E}(X|\mathcal{G}).} (2.3.4)
  3. (3)
    ​

    If 𝒢={∅︀,Ω}\mathcal{G}=\{\emptyset,\Omega\} is the trivial σ\sigma-algebra, then

    𝔼⁢(X|𝒢)=𝔼⁢(X)\boxed{\mathbb{E}(X|\mathcal{G})=\mathbb{E}(X)} (2.3.5)
